Next, the lecture covers differentiable calculus, which is crucial for training deep networks. The concept of a derivative is explained as a measure of how a function changes with respect to its input. For simple linear functions, this change is constant, but for more complex functions, the derivative gives us the slope of the tangent line at any given point. The instructor reviews several rules of differentiation: the product rule, which is used when two functions are multiplied; the quotient rule, for when one function is divided by another; and the chain rule, which applies when you have a composition of functions. The chain rule is particularly important in deep learning because neural networks are composed of multiple layers, each applying different functions. In addition to these rules, the lecture introduces the concept of partial derivatives, which are used when dealing with functions that depend on multiple variables. The collection of these partial derivatives is called the gradient, and this vector indicates the direction in which the function increases most rapidly. This gradient is used in optimization techniques like gradient descent, which is used to update the network’s parameters during training.

For further clarity, think about how the derivative of a function gives you a precise idea of how a small change in input will affect the output. In deep learning, this means that the gradient provides the direction in which you should adjust the model’s parameters to improve performance. The chain rule is especially crucial because it allows the calculation of gradients through layers of a network—a process known as backpropagation—which is the engine that drives the learning process.

To ensure that the derivatives are computed correctly, the instructor mentions numerical differentiation—a method where you approximate the derivative by looking at the change in function values when you make a small adjustment to the input. This technique is very useful for verifying that your analytical computations of derivatives are correct.

Expanding on this, numerical differentiation serves as a check against errors in your derivative formulas. Although it might not be efficient for large-scale problems, it’s a powerful tool for debugging and ensuring that the gradients calculated by your algorithms are accurate before you proceed with training your model.

These concepts are essential when constructing models that predict outcomes based on multiple variables.

To add more depth here, probability distributions help in modeling the uncertainty inherent in data.
